Anhinga
Scientific Name: Anhinga anhinga
Endangerment Status: Least Concern

The anhinga, often called the "snakebird" due to its long neck, is a skilled underwater hunter that dives to catch fish in the slow-moving waters of freshwater sloughs. Unlike other waterbirds, it lacks waterproof feathers, requiring it to spread its wings to dry after swimming.

Great Blue Heron
Scientific Name: Ardea herodias
Endangerment Status: Least Concern

The great blue heron is a wading bird that hunts in the shallow waters of freshwater sloughs, using its sharp beak to catch fish, amphibians, and small reptiles. It plays a key role in controlling fish populations and maintaining the balance of the aquatic ecosystem.

Gar Fish
Scientific Name: Lepisosteidae
Endangerment Status: Least Concern

Gars are ancient fish with armored scales and sharp teeth, acting as apex predators in the freshwater slough ecosystem by preying on smaller fish and maintaining population control. As a crucial food source for predators like the great blue heron and anhinga, they help sustain the delicate balance of the Everglades food web.

Burrowing Owl
Scientific Name: Athene cunicularia
Endangerment Status: Species of Special Concern

The burrowing owl is a ground-dwelling bird that uses abandoned burrows, often those of gopher tortoises, for nesting and shelter. By preying on insects and small vertebrates, it helps regulate local food webs, contributing to the balance of the Everglades ecosystem.

Gopher Tortoise
Scientific Name: Gopherus polyphemus
Endangerment Status: Vulnerable

The gopher tortoise is a keystone species in the Everglades, known for its burrowing behavior, which provides shelter for over 350 other species, including insects, snakes, and small mammals. By maintaining open, sandy habitats, they help promote plant growth and biodiversity, playing a crucial role in the overall health of the ecosystem.

Eastern Indigo Snake
Scientific Name: Drymarchon couperi
Endangerment Status: Threatened

The eastern indigo snake is a top predator in the Everglades, playing a vital role in controlling populations of small mammals, amphibians, and other reptiles, including venomous snakes. It often relies on gopher tortoise burrows for shelter, highlighting its close ecological relationship with this keystone species.

American Alligator
Scientific Name: Alligator mississippiensis
Endangerment Status: Least Concern

The American alligator is a keystone species in the Everglades, playing a crucial role in shaping the landscape of cypress domes by creating "alligator holes." These depressions retain water during the dry season, providing a refuge for fish, amphibians, and other wildlife. As a top predator, the alligator helps regulate populations of various species, ensuring ecological balance.

Bromeliad Plant
Scientific Name: Tillandsia
Endangerment Status: Varies by species

Bromeliads are epiphytic plants that grow on cypress trees, absorbing moisture and nutrients from the air and rain. Their cup-like structures collect water, creating microhabitats that support insects, frogs, and other small organisms.

Saltwater Crocodile
Scientific Name: Crocodylus acutus
Endangerment Status: Vulnerable

The saltwater crocodile is a top predator in the mangrove ecosystem, preying on fish, birds, and small mammals to help regulate populations and maintain ecological balance. Unlike the American alligator, it thrives in brackish and saltwater environments, making the Everglades’ mangrove forests a crucial part of its range.

Eastern Rat Snake
Scientific Name: Pantherophis alleghaniensis
Endangerment Status: Least Concern

The eastern rat snake is an adaptable predator that hunts rodents, birds, and eggs in the mangrove ecosystem, helping control prey populations. It is an excellent climber, often navigating the tangled mangrove roots and branches in search of food

Mangrove Crab
Scientific Name: Aratus pisonii
Endangerment Status: Not Evaluated

Mangrove crabs are key decomposers, feeding on fallen leaves and detritus, which helps recycle nutrients back into the ecosystem. By burrowing in the sediment and climbing tree roots, they also aid in aerating the soil and supporting the health of the mangrove habitat.

Manatee
Scientific Name: Trichechus manatus
Endangerment Status: Threatened

Manatees are gentle herbivores that graze on seagrass beds along the Everglades coastline, helping to control vegetation growth and maintain a healthy aquatic ecosystem. As they move through shallow waters, they also stir up sediment, which can redistribute nutrients and support marine life.

Roseate Spoonbill
Scientific Name: Platalea ajaja
Endangerment Status: Least Concern (Threatened in parts of the U.S. due to habitat loss)

The roseate spoonbill is a striking wading bird that uses its distinctive spoon-shaped bill to sift through shallow waters in search of crustaceans, small fish, and invertebrates. Its feeding habits help regulate prey populations and indicate the overall health of the coastal wetlands.
